Caput I

Verbs; 1st and 2nd Conjugations; Adverbs; Reading and Translating

Grammatica

Verbum, Verba (Verbs)

Given that verbs are the most important part of a sentence, we will begin by focusing on them. Verbs have these five sub-categories:

  1. Person (persōna): The subject which performs the verb. Is it 1st, 2nd or 3rd person?
  2. Number (numerus): Is the subject singular or plural?
  3. Tense (tempus): When is the action performed? in the present, future, imperfect, perfect, future perfect, or pluperfect?
  4. Mood (modus): is the verb in the indicative, imperative, or subjunctive mood?
  5. Voice (vōx): is the subject performing the action (active) or is the action happening to the subject (passive)?
Coniugātiō (Conjugation)

To conjugate (from Latin, coniugāre, to join) a finite verb (a verb that is inflected to match the person, number, tense, mood, voice) is to list all its forms. Therefore, if you were asked to conjugate laudāre in the present tense, active voice, of the indicative mood, you'd say:

Singular Plural
1st Pers. laudō laudāmus
2nd Pers. laudās laudātis
3rd Pers. laudat laudant
Personal Endings

As you can see above, each person and number has its own personal ending.

  1. -ō/-m corresponds to 1st person singular
  2. -mus corresponds to 1st person plural
  3. -s corresponds to 2nd person singular
  4. -tis corresponds to 2nd person plural
  5. -t corresponds to 3rd person singular
  6. -nt corresponds to 3rd person plural

Present Stem

In Latin, there are 4 groups of Latin verbs, separated by the stem vowel. Therefore, the 1st conjugation verbs are distinguished by -ā- as in laudāre and the 2nd conjugation verbs by -ē- as in monēre.

In order to conjugate them in the present tense, drop the -re from the infinitive forms and add the personal endings.

Hence, since we have already done the 1st conjugation verb laudāre, let's conjugate monēre: moneō, monēs, monet, monēmus, monētis, monent.

Please note that there are three ways to translate the Latin present in English. Let us take "mē monent" as an example:

  1. Simple Present: They warn me
  2. Progressive Present: They are warning me
  3. Emphatic Present: They do warn me
Present Active Imperative

This is the mood used to give commands. It exists only in the 2nd person, in either the singular or plural.

Thus you have:

  1. laudā! ; laudāte!
  2. monē! ; monēte!
Adverbium

These are words that modify a verb, an adjective, or another verb. It goes before the thing it modifies such as: Egō deum semper laudō
