Caput I

Verbs; 1st and 2nd Conjugations; Adverbs; Reading and Translating

Grammatica

Verbum, Verba (Verbs)

Given that verbs are the most important part of a sentence, we will begin by focusing on them. Verbs have these five sub-categories:

  1. Person (persōna): The subject which performs the verb. Is it 1st, 2nd or 3rd person?
  2. Number (numerus): Is the subject singular or plural?
  3. Tense (tempus): When is the action performed? in the present, future, imperfect, perfect, future perfect, or pluperfect?
  4. Mood (modus): is the verb in the indicative, imperative, or subjunctive mood?
  5. Voice (vōx): is the subject performing the action (active) or is the action happening to the subject (passive)?
Coniugātiō (Conjugation)

To conjugate (from Latin, coniugāre, to join) a finite verb (a verb that is inflected to match the person, number, tense, mood, voice) is to list all its forms. Therefore, if you were asked to conjugate laudāre in the present tense, active voice, of the indicative mood, you'd say:

Singular Plural
1st Pers. laudō laudāmus
2nd Pers. laudās laudātis
3rd Pers. laudat laudant
Personal Endings

As you can see above, each person and number has its own personal ending.

  1. -ō/-m corresponds to 1st person singular
  2. -mus corresponds to 1st person plural
  3. -s corresponds to 2nd person singular
  4. -tis corresponds to 2nd person plural
  5. -t corresponds to 3rd person singular
  6. -nt corresponds to 3rd person plural

Present Stem

In Latin, there are 4 groups of Latin verbs, separated by the stem vowel. Therefore, the 1st conjugation verbs are distinguished by -ā- as in laudāre and the 2nd conjugation verbs by -ē- as in monēre.

In order to conjugate them in the present tense, drop the -re from the infinitive forms and add the personal endings.

Hence, since we have already done the 1st conjugation verb laudāre, let's conjugate monēre: moneō, monēs, monet, monēmus, monētis, monent.

Please note that there are three ways to translate the Latin present in English. Let us take "mē monent" as an example:

  1. Simple Present: They warn me
  2. Progressive Present: They are warning me
  3. Emphatic Present: They do warn me
Present Active Imperative

This is the mood used to give commands. It exists only in the 2nd person, in either the singular or plural.

Thus you have:

  1. laudā! ; laudāte!
  2. monē! ; monēte!
Adverbium

These are words that modify a verb, an adjective, or another verb. It goes before the thing it modifies such as: Egō deum semper laudō




Caput II

First Declension Nouns and Adjectives; Prepositions; Conjunctions; Interjections

Grammatica

Nomen, nomina (Nouns)

In Latin, nouns signal their functions within a sentence via their endings, i.e. declension. These endings signal six different cases:

  1. Nominative: signals the subject of a sentence
  2. Gentive: signals the possessor of another noun
  3. Dative: signals the indirect object of a sentence
  4. Accusative: signals the direct object or the object of a preposition within a sentence
  5. Ablative: signals many different functions such as
    1. Ablative of Instrument/means: He bought the land with/by means of cattle. Answers "with what/by means of what"
    2. Ablative of Agent: He was struck by the girl. Answers "by whom"
    3. Ablative of Accompaniment: He was reading with his teacher. Answers "with whom"
    4. Ablative of Place: He is in the river. Answers "where/from which"
    5. Ablative of Time: I will be ready within one hour. Answers "when/within which"
  6. Vocative: signals the addressee
First Declension Nouns and Adjectives

Here is the paradigm for first declension nouns and adjectives. The majority of first declension nouns are feminine but a few are masculine like nauta.


Paradigm of 1st Declension nouns, feminine
Sing. Pl.
Nom. porta magna portae magnae
Gen. portae magnae portārum magnārum
Dat. portae magnae portīs magnīs
Acc. portam magnam portās magnās
Abl. portā magnā portīs magnīs
Voc. porta magna portae magnae
Paradigm of 1st Declension nouns, masculine
Sing. Pl.
Nom. nauta magnus nautae magnī
Gen. nautae magnī nautārum magnōrum
Dat. nautae magnō nautīs magnīs
Acc. nautam magnum nautās magnōs
Abl. nautā magnō nautīs magnīs
Voc. nauta magne nautae magnī
Adjectives, Agreement, and Word Order

Adjectives must agree with the noun they are modifying in gender, case, and number. As with the previous table, the ending of the adhjective does not always match the ending of the noun it is modifying.

As for word order, the adjective usually comes after the noun it is modifying. However, if the adjective denotes size or number, or if it's a demonstrative (hic, ille), it comes before the noun it modifies.




Caput III

Second Declension Masculine Nouns and Adjectives; Apposition; Word Order

Grammatica

The Second Declension

In this group, there are both masculine and neuter nouns. The masculine ones end in -us or -er in the nominative singular; the neuter in -um.


Paradigm of 2nd Declension nouns, masculine -us
Sing. Pl.
Nom. magnus numerus magnī numerī
Gen. magnī numerī magnōrum numerōrum
Dat. magnō numerō magnīs numerīs
Acc. magnum numerum magnōs numerōs
Abl. magnō numerō magnīs numerīs
Voc. magne numere magnī numerī

Paradigm of 2nd Declension nouns, masculine -er
Sing. Pl.
Nom. magnus ager magnī agrī
Gen. magnī agrī magnōrum agrōrum
Dat. magnō agrō magnīs agrīs
Acc. magnum agrum magnōs agrōs
Abl. magnō agrō magnīs agrīs
Voc. magne ager magnī agrī
Apposition

An appositive is a noun that is placed next to another noun, to give more information

Ex: Caesarem, imperatorem Romanorum, in foro video.

As you can see, the appositive matches the noun it is explaining by case and sometimes by gender and number. The appositive is usually contained in commas.

Word Order

This is the typical word order in Latin:

  1. N - the subject and its modifiers
  2. I - the indirect object and its modifiers
  3. D - the direct object and its modifiers
  4. A - Adverbs, adverbial phrases
  5. V - the main verb of the sentence

Be aware, especially in classical Latin, this word order is not set in stone; prose writers, and especially poets, are very flexible with their syntax.
